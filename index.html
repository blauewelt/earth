single day

Daily NASA GIBS imagery. Aggregate averages any continuous layer (SST & anomalies, sea ice, snow, land temperature, salinity, chlorophyll, aerosol) per pixel over the past N days ending on the chosen date (1–730) — missing pixels (clouds, night) are excluded and each pixel divides by the days it was actually observed, so cloudy-day gaps fill in. Independent of the comparison mode. Side-by-side: drag the divider (left = past, right = current). Computed difference: per-pixel "now − then" for the continuous layers except chlorophyll & aerosol (too erratic day-to-day; precipitation is excluded from both). While a window is active, layers that can't be averaged (true colour, precipitation) are hidden rather than shown as a misleading single day — a note appears below when that happens. Averaged views cap zoom to stay responsive and sample up to 12 days from the window.

⚠ Computed difference works on continuous raster layers (SST, SST anomalies, sea ice). Precipitation/aerosol are instantaneous & noisy, and point or snapshot layers — glaciers, emissions, floats, biodiversity — have no per-pixel time series to difference, so they are unaffected.

Land vs ocean warming

Land has warmed roughly twice as fast as the ocean surface. That contrast drives shifting climate zones, heatwaves, and the poleward species shifts in the Biodiversity layer — and it is why a "global" number understates what continents actually experience. For land temperature in space, turn on the Land surface temperature (MODIS) layer on the globe.

Source: NASA GISS GISTEMP v4.

Context

The AMOC carries ~17 Sv of warm water north at this latitude on average. Statistical early-warning studies (Ditlevsen & Ditlevsen 2023) and model experiments (van Westen et al. 2024) suggest weakening toward a possible tipping point — this dashboard tracks the primary observational record. Roadmap: OSNAP & Florida Current series, SST-fingerprint indices, variance / autocorrelation early-warning statistics.

Blue dots are satellite altimetry. 1900–2018 budget from Frederikse et al. 2020; altimetry from NOAA. Hover for the year-by-year breakdown.

Why this matters — budget closure

Sea-level rise is a physical accounting identity: the observed rise must equal thermal expansion of warming water plus the mass added by melting glaciers, Greenland, Antarctica, and changes in land water storage. That these independent measurements sum to the observed total ("closure") is one of the strongest cross-checks in climate science — and it decomposes why the ocean is rising: roughly a third thermal expansion, the rest added ice mass. A small residual between the observed line and the summed stack reflects remaining measurement uncertainty.

Sources: Frederikse et al. 2020, NOAA STAR altimetry.

What is this?

earth is a prototype for exploring the world's open climate data on a 3D globe — and, eventually, for generating predictions from it (e.g., will the AMOC collapse, and when?).

It renders time-dynamic NASA GIBS imagery with zero API keys, shows facility-level emissions (Climate TRACE) and the live Argo float fleet, marks the AMOC monitoring arrays, and ships a machine-readable catalog of 241 open climate datasets.

Tips

  • Zoom: scroll, pinch (touch or trackpad), or the +/− buttons
  • Compare: pick "vs N years ago" and drag the divider across the globe
  • Click any point for details; click a station marker for its data link
  • Biodiversity: pick a climate-indicator species to see its shifting range
  • Hover-and-rest (or click) the globe to read a raster layer's value at that point
